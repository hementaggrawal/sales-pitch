# Spaze App: Real Conversation Pitch Guide

## What is Spaze App?
Spaze App is a free, community-driven platform for office buildings with hundreds of companies. It helps everyone onboard, share offerings, post requests, and connect with others who can help or collaborate — breaking barriers to build a thriving business ecosystem within your building.

---

## Scenario 1: At Reception — CEO/Founder Is NOT Available

This is the most common situation. You walk in, and the receptionist or front-desk person is the gatekeeper. The goal: don't get dismissed. Plant the seed, leave something behind, and get a way back in.

---

### Case A: Receptionist says "Sir is not here" / "Ma'am is in a meeting"

> **You:** "Hi! I'm from [your company], we're in this building too. I wanted to share something quick with your founder or office head — is he/she around?"
>
> **Receptionist:** "No, sir is not in right now."
>
> **You:** "No problem at all. Actually, maybe you can help me. Do you know how many companies are in this building?"
>
> **Receptionist:** "A lot, I think..."
>
> **You:** "Yeah, over 500. And the funny thing is, most of us don't even know what the company next door does. We've built a free app called Spaze — it's like a directory + marketplace just for this building. Your company can list what you offer, and if you need any service — printing, IT support, legal, catering, anything — you can find someone right here instead of searching outside."
>
> **Receptionist:** "Okay, sounds interesting."
>
> **You:** "Can I leave this flyer with you? It has a QR code — takes 2 minutes to sign up. If your boss has a minute later, I'd love to show them a quick demo. What's a good time to come back?"
>
> **Receptionist:** "Maybe try tomorrow afternoon."
>
> **You:** "Perfect. Could I get a name so I can ask for the right person? ...Great. I'll come by tomorrow. Thanks!"

**What you achieved:** You pitched to the receptionist (they'll mention it), left material behind, and secured a follow-up time with a name.

---

### Case B: Receptionist is dismissive — "We're not interested" / "We don't need anything"

> **You:** "Hi! I'm from a company in this building — we've built a free app for all the companies here to connect."
>
> **Receptionist:** "We're not interested, thanks."
>
> **You:** "Totally understand — I'm not selling anything, I promise. It's actually a free community platform for the building. Quick question though — does your company ever need services from outside? Like printing, courier, catering, maybe a CA or legal help?"
>
> **Receptionist:** "Yeah, sometimes."
>
> **You:** "So that's exactly what this solves. There are companies in this building that do all of that — but nobody knows about each other. This app just makes everyone visible. Your company can also list what you do, and other companies here will find you. Zero cost."
>
> **Receptionist:** "Hmm, I'll tell my manager."
>
> **You:** "That would be great. Here's a flyer — the QR code is right there. And I'm in the building, so if anyone has questions, I'm easy to find. Thanks for hearing me out!"

**Key move:** You turned "not interested" around by making it about *them* getting discovered, not just using a service.

---

### Case C: Receptionist says "Leave your card / flyer, we'll call you"

> **You:** "Sure, here's the flyer. But just so whoever reads it has context — this isn't a product we're selling. It's a free app for this entire building. Every company can list what they do and find what they need."
>
> *(Hand over the flyer, then add:)*
>
> **You:** "One more thing — if your boss or office manager has literally 2 minutes, I can show them on my phone right now. It's faster than reading the flyer. But if not, I'll check back in a couple of days. What's the best day?"

**Key move:** You reframed "leave your card" from a brush-off into an actual follow-up appointment.

---

### Case D: Receptionist is friendly and curious — wants to know more

> **You:** "Hi! I'm from [your company], right here in the building. We've created a free app called Spaze for all the companies in this building."
>
> **Receptionist:** "Oh cool, what does it do?"
>
> **You:** "It's super simple. Your company signs up, lists what you offer — like if you do accounting, or marketing, or printing — and then any of the 500+ companies here can find you. And if you need something — say a web developer, or a lawyer, or bulk stationery — you post it, and someone in the building can help."
>
> **Receptionist:** "That's actually useful. We keep calling outside vendors for everything."
>
> **You:** "Exactly! And chances are, someone right here in the building does it. You just didn't know. Want me to show you on my phone? It takes 10 seconds."
>
> *(Show the app)*
>
> **You:** "See — these are companies already on it. You can browse, search, post, chat. All free."
>
> **Receptionist:** "Nice. Let me show this to my boss."
>
> **You:** "Please do! Here's the flyer. And tell your boss if they want a 2-minute demo, I'm in the building — just send someone to find me or drop a message on the app."

**Key move:** When someone is genuinely interested, SHOW the app. Visual proof beats any pitch.

## Scenario 2: The "Sit and Talk" Approach — Getting Past Small Talk Into a Real Conversation

Sometimes you get invited in, someone says "sit", or you get 5 minutes with a manager/admin. Here's how to make it count without sounding like a sales pitch.

---

### The Framework: ASK > RELATE > SHOW > ASK AGAIN

Don't start by explaining the app. Start by asking about *their* world.

---

### Full Conversation Example: You've been asked to sit down with an Office Manager

> **You:** "Thanks for the time. So, tell me — what does your company do?"
>
> **Manager:** "We're into interior design and fit-outs."
>
> **You:** "Oh nice. So you'd be working with suppliers — wood, glass, hardware, maybe electricians and painters?"
>
> **Manager:** "Yeah, we work with a lot of vendors."
>
> **You:** "Quick question — do you know if any of those vendors, or even companies that could send you clients, are actually in this building?"
>
> **Manager:** "Hmm, not really. I haven't checked."
>
> **You:** "That's the problem we keep seeing. There are 500+ companies here, and everybody's doing business with people across the city, not realizing that the perfect vendor or client might be two floors away. That's why we built Spaze."
>
> **Manager:** "What's Spaze?"
>
> **You:** "It's a free app — think of it like a business directory + marketplace, but only for this building. Every company lists what they offer and what they need. So if someone here needs interior design work, they find you. And if you need a good electrician or a painter, you might find one right here too."
>
> **Manager:** "That's interesting. But will people actually use it?"
>
> **You:** "Good question. We're going door to door right now, getting companies signed up. We already have [X] companies on it. The more that join, the more useful it becomes. It takes 2 minutes to register."
>
> **Manager:** "How does it work technically?"
>
> **You:** "Super simple — download the app, register your company, list your services, and you're live. You can also post if you need something. Other companies see it and reach out through in-app chat. No middleman, no charges."
>
> **Manager:** "Let me talk to my boss about it."
>
> **You:** "Absolutely. Here's a flyer with the QR code for when you're ready. And honestly — even if you just sign up yourself and browse, you'll be surprised what companies are in this building. Would it help if I came back for a quick 2-minute demo with your boss?"
>
> **Manager:** "Maybe, let me check when he's free."
>
> **You:** "No rush. I'm in the building, so just let me know. Thanks for the time!"

> **Them:** "Yeah."
>
> **You:** "Do you know what even 5 of them do?"
>
> **Them:** "Not really."
>
> **You:** "That's the problem. We built a free app called Spaze — every company lists what they offer and what they need. You find clients, vendors, partners right here in the building. Takes 2 minutes to sign up. No cost, no catch."
>
> *(Show the app on your phone for 10 seconds)*
>
> **You:** "See — here's what it looks like. Companies are already on here. You list your stuff, and anyone in the building who needs it finds you. Simple."
>
> **Them:** "Okay, share the link."
>
> **You:** "Here's the QR code. And if you need help setting up, I'm right here in the building."

---

### Sit-and-Talk: When They Keep Asking "But Why?" (The Deep Skeptic)

> **Them:** "Okay, but I still don't get it. Why would I use this?"
>
> **You:** "Let me give you a real example. Last week, a company on the 3rd floor needed visiting cards printed urgently. They ordered it from a shop across the city. Turns out, there's a printing company on the 6th floor. Could've walked down, gotten it done same-day, probably cheaper. They just didn't know."
>
> **Them:** "Okay, that makes sense for some people. But we're fine."
>
> **You:** "And that's fair. But flip it around — what if a company here needs exactly what *you* offer, but they're hiring someone from outside because they don't know you're here? You're losing business you don't even know about."
>
> **Them:** "...Hmm. That's a point."
>
> **You:** "That's literally all Spaze does. Makes you visible to the building. Free, 2 minutes, done."

## Golden Rules for the Sit-and-Talk

1. **Ask first, pitch second.** Start with "What does your company do?" — not "Let me tell you about our app."
2. **Make it about them.** Find their pain point (vendors, clients, visibility) and connect Spaze to it.
3. **Show, don't tell.** Open the app on your phone. 10 seconds of seeing it beats 5 minutes of explaining.
4. **Always leave something behind.** Flyer, QR code, or even a WhatsApp message with the link.
5. **Always get something back.** A name, a follow-up time, or a sign-up on the spot.
6. **Never argue.** If they say no, say "No problem — here's a flyer in case you change your mind. I'm in the building."
